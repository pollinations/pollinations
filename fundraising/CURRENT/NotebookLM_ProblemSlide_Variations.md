# Problem Slide Variations — Creator Side vs. User Side

Each variation splits the problem into two clear halves. Mix and match.

---

## Variation A: The Paywall and the Subscription Tax

**Creator side:** AI APIs require a credit card before you build anything real. Free tiers last minutes. A teenager with a great idea hits a wall before finishing a prototype. The ones who push through self-limit — cap users, skip features, never promote — because every user costs them money.

**User side:** Every AI micro-app wants its own subscription. $10/month for a calorie tracker. $10/month for a bedtime story bot. $10/month for a study helper. Nobody signs up. The long tail of AI apps dies on the vine because individual subscriptions don't work for small tools.

**Visual:** Split screen. Left: creator at a pixel computer, a padlock over the API, empty wallet. Right: user buried under a pile of subscription invoices, drowning in $10 bills.

---

## Variation B: Fear of Success

**Creator side:** The vibe coding era means anyone can build an AI app in an afternoon. But the better your app gets, the more it costs you. Creators don't go broke — they never try. They self-censor: build small, share quietly, kill features that use expensive models. The best AI apps are the ones that were never released.

**User side:** Meanwhile, users are stuck paying separately for every tool they touch. One wallet for ChatGPT, another for Midjourney, another for that indie app they used twice. Subscription fatigue means they stop discovering new apps entirely.

**Visual:** Split screen. Left: creator with a great app behind glass, a "too expensive to share" sign. Right: user's phone screen covered in subscription badges, finger hovering but not tapping.

---

## Variation C: The Electricity Bill Problem

**Creator side:** Imagine opening a restaurant where you pay the customer's electricity bill. That's every AI platform today. The creator builds the app, the user enjoys it, and the creator gets the bill. So creators keep the lights dim — fewer features, fewer users, smaller ambitions.

**User side:** Now imagine paying a separate electric company for every appliance you own. One bill for your fridge, one for your lamp, one for your toaster. That's subscription hell — and it's why users won't adopt AI micro-apps one by one.

**Visual:** Split screen styled as two utility bills. Left: "Creator's Monthly AI Bill — $2,847 — for an app 50,000 people love." Right: "User's Monthly Subscriptions — 14 apps × $10 = $140 — for tools used 3 times each."

## Variation D: Two Ceilings

**Creator side:** There's an access ceiling and a scale ceiling. Access: most AI APIs gate behind payment — the creator pool is artificially small. Scale: creators who do build something limit it deliberately. They add usage caps, remove features, avoid going viral. Growing your app means growing your debt.

**User side:** There's a commitment ceiling. Users won't subscribe to apps they might use once a week. Micro-apps need micro-payments — but every platform forces monthly subscriptions. The result: users stick to 2-3 big platforms and ignore everything else.

**Visual:** Two glass ceilings, one above a creator reaching up, one above a user reaching up. Both blocked. A crack forming where Pollen breaks through both.

## Variation E: The Numbers

**Creator side:** Average free tier on a major AI API: ~$5 of credits. Cost to run a useful app for 1,000 users/month: ~$200-500. Gap between "I can try this" and "I can sustain this": infinite. Result: creators build demos, not products.

**User side:** Average person's AI subscriptions: 3-5 at $10-20/month each. Actual usage: concentrated in 1-2 apps. Wasted spend: 60%+. Willingness to add another $10/month subscription for a micro-app: near zero.

**Visual:** Two bar charts side by side. Left: tiny green bar (free tier) next to tall red bar (cost to run an app). Right: stack of subscription bars with most grayed out as "unused."

## Variation F: Before and After (Dual)

**Creator before:** Has an idea → signs up for API → runs out of free credits in a day → needs credit card → builds a limited prototype → can't afford to share it → project dies in a folder.

**Creator after:** Has an idea → gets free daily credits on Pollinations → builds and iterates → ships it → users pay with Pollen → creator earns → builds more.

**User before:** Finds a cool AI app → asked to subscribe $10/month → thinks "I'd use this twice a week" → closes tab → forgets it exists.

**User after:** Finds a cool AI app → pays 20 Pollen for this session → loves it → finds another app → same Pollen balance works there too → discovers 10 more apps that month.

**Visual:** Four-panel comic strip, pixel art. Two "before" panels (gray, frustrated) and two "after" panels (colorful, happy). Creator and user paths shown in parallel.

## Variation G: The Missing Middle

**Creator side:** Big companies have budgets. Hobbyists are fine with free tiers. But the middle — the serious indie creator who could build the next great AI app — has no path. Too ambitious for free tiers, too small for enterprise pricing. This is where the best apps would come from, and it's exactly where the current ecosystem fails.

**User side:** Power users will pay for ChatGPT Pro. Casual users won't pay for anything. But the middle — someone who'd gladly spend $5-20/month across 10 different micro-apps — has no way to do that without 10 separate subscriptions. The economics of small, frequent, cross-app spending don't exist yet.

**Visual:** A gap in the middle of a bridge. Creators on one side, users on the other. Pollen forming the missing bridge segment connecting them.
